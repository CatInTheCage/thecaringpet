New Puppy Essentials Checklist
Your first 30 days with a new puppy — printable guide

Before You Bring Your Puppy Home

Food & Water

  • Puppy food (complete & balanced for growth)
  • Stainless steel food & water bowls
  • Soft training treats
  • Slow feeder (if puppy eats too fast)

Crate & Bedding

  • Crate with a movable divider
  • Washable crate pad or blanket
  • Exercise pen (optional)

Potty Training

  • Enzyme cleaner
  • Potty pads (optional)
  • Waste bags

Collar, Leash & ID

  • Flat collar + ID tag
  • 4–6 ft leash (no retractable)
  • No-pull harness
  • Microchip registered to you

Toys & Chews

  • Teething chew (puppy-safe)
  • KONG Puppy (stuffable, freezable)
  • Puzzle or treat-dispensing toy
  • Fetch & tug toys

Grooming & Health

  • Nail grinder or clippers
  • Soft brush
  • Dog toothbrush + enzymatic toothpaste
  • Vet wellness exam scheduled (week 1)
Quick tip: Start with the same food the breeder or shelter used and transition to a new brand gradually over 7–10 days. Look for an AAFCO statement confirming the food is "complete and balanced for growth."

The 3-3-3 Rule
What to expect in your puppy's first 3 months
3 Days — Decompress
Your puppy is overwhelmed by a brand-new world. Give them a small, quiet safe space.
  • Keep in crate or pen in one calm room
  • Let the puppy exit the crate on their own
  • No forced interaction or visitors
  • Same food, fresh water; reduced appetite is normal
3 Weeks — Learn the Routine
Consistency clicks. The puppy learns when meals and potty breaks happen.
  • Set predictable feeding, potty, play & sleep times
  • Begin short positive-reinforcement training
  • Housetraining starts to stick
  • Start gentle handling (paws, ears, mouth)
3 Months — Feel at Home
The puppy treats the home as territory and shows their true personality.
  • Reliable housetraining rhythm forms
  • Comfortable with family members
  • Continue vaccination series & socialization
  • Discuss spay/neuter timing with your vet
Remember: The 3-3-3 rule is a widely shared framework used by shelters and rescues — a helpful guide, not a clinical deadline. Every puppy adjusts at their own pace. Let yours build confidence gradually.

When to Call the Vet Immediately

  • Refusal to eat or drink for more than 12 hours
  • Repeated vomiting, or diarrhea lasting more than a day (especially with blood)
  • Lethargy, weakness, or difficulty breathing
  • Straining to urinate/defecate, or no urination
Poison emergency? Call the ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center at (888) 426-4435 (24/7) if your puppy eats chocolate, xylitol, grapes, rat poison, or human medication.
