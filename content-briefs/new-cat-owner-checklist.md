### H2: What Is the 3-3-3 Rule for Cats? (≈400 words)

#### H3: 3 Days to Decompress
- Create a safe room
- Minimize visitors and noise
- Let the cat hide; do not force interaction
- Keep food, water, litter box in the same room

#### H3: 3 Weeks to Settle
- Gradual exploration beyond safe room
- Routine establishment (feeding times, play times)
- Bonding through play and treats
- Watch for emerging personality

#### H3: 3 Months to Fully Trust
- Cat feels like part of the family
- Full territory access (if appropriate)
- Behavioral patterns stable
- When to consider introducing other pets

#### H3: Why the Rule Matters
- Reduces stress-related behaviors
- Prevents premature rehoming
- Sets realistic expectations

#### H3: Set Up a Safe Room
- Quiet room with door that closes
- Food/water/litter arranged in a triangle
- Hiding spots at different heights

#### H3: Cat-Proof the Whole House
- Secure windows and balconies
- Hide electrical cords
- Remove toxic plants (lilies, pothos, etc.)
- Store cleaning supplies and medications

#### H3: Create Vertical Space
- Cats need to climb and observe

### H2: The First 24 Hours: What to Expect (≈300 words)
- Bring cat home in carrier; do not open in car
- Open carrier in safe room; let cat exit on their own
- Food and water available but do not force eating
- Litter box placed and shown gently
- Expect hiding, reduced appetite, or silence

- Letting the cat roam the whole house immediately
- Forcing affection or picking up too soon
- Buying the wrong litter box size
- Feeding dog food or too many treats
- Skipping the vet visit
- Introducing to other pets too quickly
- Punishing fear-based behaviors

- Not eating for more than 24 hours
- No urination in 24 hours or straining in litter box
- Diarrhea or vomiting lasting more than a day
- Lethargy, hiding, or sudden behavior change
- Difficulty breathing

## 3. FAQ List (for `FAQ.astro` component)

1. **What is the 3-3-3 rule for cats?**
   - 3 days to decompress, 3 weeks to settle, 3 months to fully trust. It is a guideline used by shelters to set realistic expectations for newly adopted cats.

2. **What should I buy before adopting a cat?**
   - Food and water bowls, litter box and litter, cat food, carrier, bed/hiding spot, scratching post, toys, collar with ID, and enzyme cleaner.

3. **How long should I keep a new cat in one room?**
   - Most cats benefit from 3–7 days in a safe room. Confident cats may be ready sooner; shy cats may need 1–2 weeks.

4. **Should I leave my new cat alone on the first night?**
   - Yes. Give them space to explore the safe room quietly. Sleep nearby if possible, but avoid forcing interaction.

5. **Why is my new cat hiding and not eating?**
   - This is usually normal stress behavior. Ensure food, water, and litter are accessible. If they do not eat within 24 hours, contact a veterinarian.

6. **Can I let my new cat sleep in my bed right away?**
   - Let the cat choose. Some cats seek closeness immediately; others need days or weeks. Never force them.

7. **How do I introduce my new cat to other pets?**
   - Wait at least 1–2 weeks. Use scent swapping, barrier feeding, and supervised short meetings. Never rush the process.

8. **What type of litter box is best for a new cat?**
   - A large, uncovered box with low sides is usually best. Place it in a quiet, accessible location.

9. **Should I change my new cat's food right away?**
   - No. Feed the same food the shelter or breeder used, then transition gradually over 7–10 days to avoid stomach upset.

10. **How can I tell if my new cat is happy?**
    - Look for relaxed body language, eating/drinking normally, using the litter box, exploring, and playful behavior. Reference `/blog/cat-body-language-guide/`.

11. **Is it normal for a new cat to hiss at me?**
    - Yes, hissing is fear communication, not aggression. Give the cat space and let them approach you.

12. **When should I take my new cat to the vet?**
    - Schedule a wellness exam within the first week. Go sooner if you notice signs of illness such as refusal to eat, vomiting, diarrhea, or lethargy.
